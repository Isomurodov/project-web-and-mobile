Quantum Computing

Introduction

”Recently, miniaturization of transistors, detectors, and, at the same time, access to extremely low temperatures (such as −273C) have opened the possibility to operate, observe, and, most importantly, isolate individual particles with their inherently quantum properties.Quantum mechanics has already led to dramatic advances in a few specific areas in medicine (e.g., MRI, laser surgery) with potential to revolutionize medical research and clinical care. Because modern computers have largely saturated computational power and no longer grow exponentially as occurred in the last century, QC, if realized, is the most promising technology for major advancements in processes.”

Solenov, D., Brieler, J., & Scherrer, J. F. (2018). The Potential of Quantum Computing and Machine Learning to Advance Clinical Research and Change the Practice of Medicine. Missouri medicine, 115(5), 463–467

Properties

”All currently known and beneficial quantum algorithms that can run on QC are based on the ability of the quantum system, upon certain rearrangement, to behave in unison. The system of many (quantum) particles, each holding a bit of information, behave as one – a property based on so-called quantum entanglement. For this reason, large chunks of data can be processed at once, operating essentially on only a few particles, that is, in a massively parallel manner. This will allow tasks that would require centuries of computing on a standard computer to require only a few minutes on a quantum computer.”

Solenov, D., Brieler, J., & Scherrer, J. F. (2018). The Potential of Quantum Computing and Machine Learning to Advance Clinical Research and Change the Practice of Medicine. Missouri medicine, 115(5), 463–467.

Qubit

”A computational quantum particle – qubit – represented as a unit (Bloch) sphere. Unlike regular bits that take values of 0 or 1, a qubit can take any values on a sphere, as represented by two angles. The poles correspond to classical 0/1 bit values.”

Solenov, D., Brieler, J., & Scherrer, J. F. (2018). The Potential of Quantum Computing and Machine Learning to Advance Clinical Research and Change the Practice of Medicine. Missouri medicine, 115(5), 463–467.

Applications

”Billions of dollars are now pouring into quantum computing. [...] The biggest promise of such computers lies in solving large combinatorics problems. [...] cybersecurity, chemical engineering, banking and finance, and advanced manufacturing suggest that these problems are common and occur in a wide range of industries. In order to assess where opportunities lie in any industry, the first step is to identify combinatorics problems that could generate substantial value if unlocked. [...] Quantum computers are not yet available at the level of power and reliability needed for solving these types of problems. Still, we highlighted three near-term commercial opportunities from recent advances in quantum hardware and software: (1) quantum-safe encryption on classical computers, (2) material and drug discovery, and (3) new quantum-inspired algorithms for classical computers”

Bova, F., Goldfarb, A. & Melko, R.G. Commercial applications of quantum computing. EPJ Quantum Technol. 8, 2 (2021). https://doi.org/10.1140/epjqt/s40507-021-00091-1

Limitations

”A key challenge for quantum computers is to provide and maintain isolation of individual quantum particles involved in computation. Extreme and stable cooling is required to make wire circuits (or circuits made of other materials) behave in a quantum fashion.”

Solenov, D., Brieler, J., & Scherrer, J. F. (2018). The Potential of Quantum Computing and Machine Learning to Advance Clinical Research and Change the Practice of Medicine. Missouri medicine, 115(5), 463–467.
